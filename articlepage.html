By Shivansh Singh 2024

The problem with right-leaning Indian historians

When this question is seen from the bird's eye view, we comprehend that Indian history has become biased from, not only political ends but also, miscellaneous other socio-economic existing differences, rather causing deep quandary in the mind of readers about what is correct and what is not.

If the readers may allow, I would like to divide the issue into 3 sub-points:-

The issue mentioned here has a two-sided nature. Just as a two-sided coin, this issue, too, has two faces. And to add, it is quite similar to the other situations. The first one is the approach of the right-wing to history. As to explain, there is to doubt, right-wing history has been neglected over time, by both, the education system and the intellectual or expert society. The education system, largely, rejected it, or for as they thought it was biased to follow facts from any side, though, the history presented was still incomplete. At the same time, the intellectual society claims that right-wing historians lack the basic knowledge of history, which should have been largely left to the reader's interpretation. To promote unbiased and expert opinions, they also neglect the Right Wing History. To be honest, the opinions may be unbiased and intellectual, but at the same time, they ignore many essential facts from both political ends, harming the very basic right of the reader; Knowledge. All the statements mentioned here apply to any biased opinion, irrespective of its political end.

Interpretation must be left to the reader. India has given the right to express itself to all of its citizens. I would like to quote a very prolific historian, Ramchandra Guha. He writes, in his book, India after Gandhi,” Those who write contemporary history know that the reader is not a passive whistle to receive the text place before him or her. The reader is also a citizen, a critical citizen, with individual political and ideological preferences. These preferences direct and dictate the reader’s view of the past and leaders and lawmakers most particularly… The farther back we go in time the less of a problem this is. Historians of the 18th century interpret and understand that time, and so, following them, do the readers. A biographer of Jefferson Napoleon can count on more trusting readers- they do not presume to know the things those men did or wish they should have done them differently. Here, the reader is usually happy to be led and guided by the expert. But the biography of John F. Kennedy or Charles de Gaulle is not so fortunate. Some, perhaps many, potential readers already know the 'truth' about these men, and are less willing to hear alternative of it, even if they're backed up with copious footnotes.”

The historian has the freedom of expression that all responsible citizens of India have. The historian has his preferences, sources and footnotes. We need to respect his opinion. I would like to quote Ramchandra Guha, again, from the same book. He writes,” The scholar who chooses to write on the Vietnam War already has strong views on the topic. The scholar who writes on the American Civil War would have less strong views and one who writes on the Revolutionary War weaker views still. For the historian as well as the citizen, the closer one gets to the present, the more judgemental one tends to become.”

Conclusion: India is a pluralist society. It is good to have different views, with due regard to India’s diversity. We must respect every opinion and try not to ignore any part of the history or view. The expert opinion is a must. We must give historians free space to write, with reliable sources. We must give a constructive relatable amount of criticism, respecting the historian’s room for error. The multi-views on one topic help the reader gain versatile and detailed knowledge. The reader’s discretion and interpretation must be respected and not be manipulated for personal gain or propaganda.
